Historical Roots: Why Hijama Has Stood the Test of Time

The earliest evidence of cupping dates to ancient Egypt around 1550 BCE in the Ebers Papyrus. Ancient Chinese medicine independently developed cupping with bamboo cups. In ancient Greece, Hippocrates himself advocated cupping for musculoskeletal pain and internal diseases. Ibn Sina (Avicenna), the genius of Islamic medicine, devoted extensive sections of his Canon of Medicine to cupping therapy.

One of the most remarkable facts: cupping arose independently in multiple civilizations simultaneously — Egypt, China, Greece and Indigenous Americas — with no direct communication between them. When a therapy is universally discovered across all of human history, that is evidence of genuine physiological efficacy.

Spiritual Reasons: Hijama as Sunnah of the Prophet ﷺ

For Muslims, hijama carries profound spiritual significance. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ practiced and recommended hijama on multiple occasions, recorded in authentic hadith. This makes it a Sunnah — a prophetic practice blessed with spiritual reward alongside physical benefit.

Recommended Hijama Days

The Prophet ﷺ recommended hijama on the 17th, 19th and 21st of the Islamic lunar month. Many practitioners align their sessions with these blessed days for maximum spiritual and physical benefit.

For Muslim practitioners and patients, hijama is not merely a health treatment — it is an act of following the Sunnah. This dual spiritual-physical dimension is one of the most powerful reasons why hijama continues to grow in Muslim-majority communities across India and the world.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is hijama painful?

Most people describe it as a strong pulling sensation, not pain. Dry cupping is generally painless. Wet cupping involves minor incisions but most patients report it as very tolerable.

How often should you do hijama?

For healthy individuals, once every 1–3 months is typical. For chronic conditions, your practitioner may recommend more frequent sessions initially.

Who should not do hijama?

Those on blood thinners, pregnant women, people with bleeding disorders or active infections should consult a qualified practitioner before hijama. Always seek professional advice.
