Hijama Research: What Modern Science Says

The scientific study of hijama cupping has accelerated significantly since 2010, with peer-reviewed papers published in journals including Evidence-Based Complementary and Alternative Medicine, the Journal of Traditional Chinese Medicine, and the American Journal of Chinese Medicine. The evidence base is still growing, but several well-designed trials have demonstrated statistically significant effects for hijama in specific conditions.

Randomised controlled trials have shown significant pain reduction in chronic neck pain, chronic lower back pain and knee osteoarthritis. Physiological studies have demonstrated increases in local blood flow of 200–400% following dry cupping, and significant decreases in serum inflammatory markers (including C-reactive protein and interleukin-6) following wet cupping sessions. Neurological studies suggest hijama activates the same descending pain-inhibition pathways that explain acupuncture's analgesic effects.

We encourage practitioners to engage with this emerging research base — not to replace traditional knowledge, but to complement it. When a patient asks 'Does hijama really work, doctor?', the ability to explain the physiological mechanisms and cite clinical trial evidence is enormously valuable in building patient confidence and expanding your practice.
